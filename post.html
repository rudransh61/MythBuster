1+2+3.....

12-23-2023 - Rudransh Bharwdaj

There is a very famous mathematical argument that $$ 1+2+3..... = -\frac{1}{12}=-0.08333333333333..$$ given by Ramanuja also known as "Ramanuja summation". But many people interpret this wrongly , For example a famous maths channel on youtube name "NumberPhile" proved this """correct""" statement wrongly . The speaker says that the Numberphile video made 3 wrong statements:

  • The infinite series $$ 1 + 2 + 3 + ...$$ actually sums to positive infinity, not $$-\frac{1}{12}$$.
  • The way the Numberphile video presents summing infinite series is not mathematically rigorous.
  • There is no such thing as “super summing” that can make divergent infinite series converge.
So , Lets deep dive into this problem and world of series in maths...........
A series is a type of sequence with meaningful order like $$ 1,2,3,4,... $$ or $$ 1,3,5,7,9... $$ or $$ 2,3,5,7,11 $$ or $$ 4,9,16...$$ etc. There is a long history of calculating sum of series and playing with them. And according to there sum , there are 2 types of series diverging and converging series
Diverging (sequence with sum tending to infinity ): From Medieval Latin dīvergō (“bend away from, go in a different direction”), from Latin dī- + vergō (“bend”). Converging (sequence with sum tending to some finite number ): traces back to the Latin word vergere, meaning “to bend or to turn.
So , this was some information about series in maths Lets look at a very famous mathematical function called zeta function
$$ \zeta (s) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^s} $$ and $$ s>1 $$
But many mathematicians tried to expand its domain to Real Number or to all complex numbers which Riemann hypothesis is about (not related to this topic). But there is a problem , because while expanding the domain , it cannot be defined for all Real Numbers <1 .... So , we have to apply analytical continuation , which means we have to complete the graph by analyzing the known values creating a smooth differentiable curve . And the equation for that curve is , $$ \zeta(1-s) = 2^{1-s} \pi^{-s} cos(\frac{\pi s}{2}) \Gamma(s) \zeta(s) $$ And we know that , $$ \zeta(-1) = 1+2+3....... (but it does not exists) $$ But if you use our New equation, you can find its value (which is the value of ** $$\zeta(-1)=-\frac{1}{12}$$ **) So,.... zeta of -1 is not 1+2+3....... its analytically equivalent to $$-\frac{1}{12}$$

Also before writing this article i found no original paper of Ramanuja proofing 1+2+3+4.... = -1/12

The original paper by Srinivasa Ramanujan that proves the analytical expression for the value of zeta(-1) is titled "A Proof of Certain Identities in Combinatory Analysis" and was published in the Proceedings of the Cambridge Philosophical Society, Vol. 16, No. 5 (1910), pp. 350-357. Mathematics
